**Tanya:** Last time you left me with something that stuck , that when motivation is actually there for you, it feels energizing, powerful, normal, and easy. That phrase has a lot in it. What's been alive for you since then?

**Client:** I feel like I lean heavy on teaching vs coaching because it’s so easy for me to hear someone tell me about their problem and within their tone and language I can easily diagnose what their mind is doing and the underlaying concept on shifting their pattern and so I often go right into telling them. I get amazing feedback but I am questioning the effectiveness of transformation versus coaching by asking questions for them to discover but that feels like a long way to getting them to knowing the mechanics at play and so it feels faster and more direct just to get to it.

**Tanya:** That lands. You're good at it, and it works on the surface. The feedback confirms that.

Here's what I want you to sit with though. When you hand someone the diagnosis, they get the insight. When they discover it themselves, they get the *experience of being someone who can see their own patterns.* That's a different thing entirely. One builds understanding. The other builds identity.

The question isn't really speed. What do you think your clients are actually walking away with when you do it your way?

**Client:** But if I played devils advocate, who’s to say that they don’t get the experience of being someone who can see their own patterns just because it didn’t come from them, discovering it themselves. If I tell them the pattern they can still experience seeing it. When they do it my way I think they are walking away with knowledge and knowledge is power because you have awareness.

**Tanya:** That's a fair push. And you're not wrong that awareness landed is still awareness.

Here's what I'd ask you to consider though. When you hand someone the pattern, their brain's job becomes "do I agree with this?" When they discover it, their brain's job is "what is actually true for me?" Those are different cognitive moves. One is reception. The other is excavation. And excavation builds the muscle they'll need when you're not in the room.

The real question isn't whether they get the insight. It's whether they get the *capacity* to find the next one on their own. What do you think happens to that capacity when the answer keeps coming from you?

**Client:** Yes that makes sense. It just feels so painfully slow because sometimes they miss the insight altogether and it feels like lost opportunity to learn.

**Tanya:** That tension is real. And honestly, it makes sense that it feels that way for someone who sees it so fast.

But what if them missing it isn't a lost opportunity? What if it's actually the most important data you have about where they really are?

**Client:** Well it may be data on where they are which only increases my pull to want to make them aware

**Tanya:** Right, and that pull, that's yours. Not theirs.

What would it mean about the client if they left a session without the insight you could see?

**Client:** I feel like it means that they continue to suffer

**Tanya:** Mm.

And what does it mean about you if they leave without it?

**Client:** I don’t think it means anything about me other than I feel frustrated that they didn’t get the insight they could have

**Tanya:** Okay. Stay with that frustration for a second.

Is it possible that some of what's driving the urge to hand them the answer is actually your own discomfort with watching them stay stuck, not just care for them?

**Client:** Yes but only because I’m watching them in discomfort knowing I can just hand them insight that’s helpful

**Tanya:** That's honest. And that care is real.

But here's what I want you to see. When you relieve *your* discomfort by handing them the answer, you may actually be interrupting the exact moment their nervous system was about to do its own work. The discomfort they're in isn't always something to rescue them from. Sometimes it's the thing that's about to crack open into their own discovery.

The frustration you feel watching them sit in it, that's actually a sign you're fully present with them. That's not a problem. The question is what you do with it.

What would it look like for you to trust the discomfort a little more, both theirs and yours?

**Client:** It would look like me asking more questions rather than telling more answers

**Tanya:** Yeah. That's it.

And notice, you just diagnosed your own pattern and generated your own answer. I didn't hand it to you. How does that land differently than if I had just told you that at the start?

**Client:** It doesn’t really do much for me because it’s what I stated as my original issue basically that I’m struggling with teaching(telling) vs coaching(asking). Nothing was really discovered.

**Tanya:** Fair. That's honest and I'll take it.

So let me ask you something different. You've been coaching long enough to have seen both ways work. Have you ever had a moment where a client discovered something themselves that genuinely surprised you, something you wouldn't have diagnosed for them?

**Client:** No

**Tanya:** Never?

**Client:** Not that I can recall but to be fair that may also be because mostly I’m teaching vs coaching so the opportunity is lower for that to happen. But even when clients are agreeing with insight shared and they relate it to their situation, nothing ever surprises me

**Tanya:** That's worth sitting with.

If nothing your clients discover ever surprises you, what does that tell you about how much space you're actually leaving for them to go somewhere you haven't already mapped?

**Client:** Are you saying that it’s impossible for me to have the full map of transformation

**Tanya:** Not impossible. But I am saying this: the map you have is yours. Built from your patterns, your wounds, your way of seeing.

Your client's interior is a different territory entirely. And the parts of them that are most alive, most specific, most true, those live in places your map doesn't cover. Not because your map is wrong. Because it's yours.

What would have to be true for you to believe a client could show you something about themselves that you genuinely couldn't have predicted?

**Client:** No the map I’m talking about is not mine. It’s the map I discovered that is universal in transformation across modalities.

**Tanya:** Okay, say more about that.

**Client:** When I’m teaching I’m sharing concepts and tools that are in neuroscience, psychology, Law of attraction, energy work that all of the major players are using. We all just have our own language in how we articulate it but all the same mechanics

**Tanya:** That's real, and I don't disagree that the mechanics are largely consistent across modalities.

But here's what I'm curious about. The map describes how transformation works. It doesn't tell you where *this* person is inside it, what specific thought is running their pattern, what the wound underneath their wound is, what metaphor is going to unlock something for them, or what they're about to say next.

The mechanics are universal. The human is not.

So when you say nothing surprises you, is it possible what you mean is the *mechanism* never surprises you, but you're collapsing that into thinking the *person* can't either?

**Client:** Yes however h the is is what I mean when I say that when they are in a session and start telling me about their problem, I am able to see where they are inside it and what specific thoughts they express that show me that.  Since the mechanism doesn’t surprise me, the person doesn’t either because they may use different words than another person but the experience of the problem always falls within the mechanics of the cycle of  transformation. They have different stories but underneath them all the core wounds with people are always the same few major wounds and diagnosis.

**Tanya:** That's a sharp diagnostic mind. And you're right.

Here's what I want to offer gently. There's a difference between being able to *see* where someone is, and that person being able to *arrive* at what they see themselves. Those are two different events. And only one of them changes them at the identity level.

You can be right about everything you're seeing and still have the client leave unchanged. Not because the insight was wrong. Because insight that arrives from outside lands in the mind. Insight that a person generates themselves lands in the body. That's where identity actually shifts.

So the question isn't whether your map is accurate. It clearly is. The question is what you believe your role is once you've read the room.

**Client:** Are you there?

**Tanya:** Yes, right here.

Yes that’s it. That makes sense that even if I have that insight that them
Discovering it on their own is what changes them. Just feels frustrating because that’s a slower process. Or maybe it’s frustrating because it’s so easy to just tell them and coaching through questions feels harder and not a honed skill as much

**Tanya:** That's real. And that second part, that's the honest one.

It's not just that telling is faster. Telling is where you're skilled. Coaching through questions is where you're still building. And sitting in that learning space, knowing the answer and having to hold it, that's frustrating in a specific way.

What would it mean about you if you sat with a client, saw exactly where they were, and asked a question instead of telling them?

**Client:** Would mean I can coach masterfully. Just sometimes I can’t formulate a question that is clean and concise and fitting. How do I develop that skill. Not even sure the best formula for forming questions.

**Tanya:** The best questions don't come from a formula. They come from genuine curiosity about the one thing the client just said that you don't fully understand yet.

Most people think they need to construct the perfect question. What actually works is simpler: find the word or phrase in what they just said that carries the most weight, and ask about that word. Just that word. "You said 'stuck', what does stuck actually feel like for you right now?" That's it. The precision comes from listening, not from constructing.

The other move is consequence. Instead of asking what someone is feeling, ask what it would mean about them if the thing they fear actually happened. That one question cuts through more surface in thirty seconds than five minutes of exploring the story.

What's the moment in a session where you feel it most, where you know a question is needed but nothing clean comes?

**Client:** After they’ve just thought dumped about their situation explaining and justifying to me everything that happened

**Tanya:** Yes. That's the hardest moment because there's so much material it feels like you have to address all of it. So you either summarize everything back or you teach into it because that at least feels organized.

But here's what's actually happening in that thought dump. They're not giving you information. They're showing you what they're most afraid to look at directly. The details are the armor. The question isn't about any of the details.

The one move that works every time after a thought dump: ignore the content entirely and go to the feeling underneath all of it. "After everything you just shared, what's the feeling that's sitting heaviest right now?" 

That question does two things. It signals that you heard them without having to prove it by recapping everything. And it cuts straight past the story into the actual work.

What happens in your body in that moment when they're thought dumping? What are you doing internally while they're talking?

**Client:** Yes, that makes sense because normally when their thought dumping, I’m trying to record notes to go back to address the details

**Tanya:** That's exactly where the skill lives.

While you're recording, you're in your head. And the question that lands, the one that actually opens something, comes from being fully in *them*. It comes from tracking the feeling underneath the words, not the words themselves.

What if you let the details go entirely? Not one note. Just listening for the one moment in the dump where their voice changes, or the one phrase they say that carries more weight than everything around it. That's the thread. That's the only thing worth pulling.

The details will come back if they matter. The emotional opening won't wait.

What would it take for you to trust that enough to try it?

**Client:** I can try it

**Tanya:** Good. And notice what I just did there, by the way.

I didn't give you a framework for how to listen. I asked you one question and you found your own answer. That's the whole thing. That's coaching through questions.

You just experienced it from the inside.

**Client:** OK, but I am gonna ask you to give me some answers because I’d love you to give me a few questions that I can ask after someone thought dumps dislike the one you said about getting to the feeling that feels heavy so that I have a few on top of my mind that I can ask as defaults

**Tanya:** Here are a few that cut through the content and go straight to what matters:

"After everything you just shared, what's the feeling that's sitting heaviest right now?"

"If you took all of that away, what's the one thing underneath it all that you're most afraid is true?"

"What's the part of that you haven't said out loud yet?"

"What do you most want me to understand about all of that?"

That last one is underrated. It hands them the pen. Whatever they say next is the real thing, not the dump.

Which of those feels most like you?

**Client:** I like the first in the last

**Tanya:** Good instincts. Those two work together actually.

Start with the last one, "what do you most want me to understand," right after the dump. It shows you heard them without recapping anything. And whatever they say, follow it with the first one. "And what's the feeling that's sitting heaviest in all of that?"

Two questions. That's a sequence you can run every time.

## The Deeper Issue
*What's really underneath the surface problem.*

- The motivation gap may not be a discipline problem — it may be a power or worth problem. When someone can't move toward something they say they want, it often signals a hidden conflict (fear of failure, belief it won't work, or that they don't deserve the outcome).
- Session 1 opened a thread: the client has a felt sense of what motivation feels like — "energizing and powerful and normal and easy." The gap is between knowing that felt sense and accessing it consistently.
- Session 2 added a layer: the client's attachment to the teaching/telling role mirrors something about their relationship to competence and identity. They are most comfortable in positions of mastery.
- Session 4 sharpens the picture: the through-line is now visible. The client is caught in a closed loop — the only evidence that would prove the new identity requires tolerating imperfection, but tolerating imperfection produces dissatisfaction, and dissatisfaction is interpreted as confirmation that they haven't changed. The loop is self-sealing. The client named this clearly: "Conscious choice is saying that's not what I want because I feel less satisfied." They are not avoidant or anxious in the clinical sense — they are a high-standard performer whose current system is unsustainable and who has no felt path to a different one.
- Critical distinction established in Session 4: the client explicitly rejected the "not enough as a person" framing — "I think I'm definitely enough but I can think that and still not be satisfied with an outcome." The pain is competence-specific, not worth-level. This distinction matters for how Tanya enters the wound.

# Session 3 — JC

*Date: 2026-04-11*

---

**JC:** I’m at my house

**Tanya:** Hey! What's on your mind today?

**JC:** Honestly, I’ve been feeling really stuck like what I want but every time I try to move toward it something stops can’t tell if it’s fear or just I don’t know. Maybe I’m not as ready as I think I am.

**Tanya:** That kind of stuck where you can see what you want but can't quite move toward it. What does stuck actually feel like for you right now?

**JC:** It feels like standing at the edge of a diving board. Like I can see the water. I know how to swim, but my feet just won’t leave the board and the longer I stand there the more people I imagine are watching and judging, even though I don’t even know if it’s anyone actually watching.

**Tanya:** That makes sense - when you've been standing at the edge long enough, your brain starts writing a story about all the eyes on you. What is it you're trying to move toward that's got you frozen on that diving board?

**JC:** I want to leave my job and start my own thing. I’ve had this idea for like two years now and I just… Keep doing it. I tell myself it’s not the right time. I need more savings. I need to learn more but honestly, I think I just don’t trust myself to pull it off.

**Tanya:** Two years is a long time to carry an idea that keeps calling you back. What would it mean about you if you tried this thing and it didn't work out the way you hoped?

**JC:** That’s the question isn’t it? I think it would mean I was fooling myself like that version of me who thought she could do. This was just naïve and then I have to go back back to the job back to the same life and everyone would know I tried and failed that feels worse than never trying it all.

**Tanya:** That's real - the story your brain is writing is that trying and failing exposes something broken about you. But let me ask you this: what if the circumstance is neutral and it's your thought about what failure would mean that's creating this paralysis?

Here's what I want you to consider - you've been treating this like there are only two outcomes: wild success or devastating failure that proves you were wrong about yourself. But what if there's a third option? What if trying teaches you something about who you're becoming, regardless of the result?

What would change if you saw this as data collection instead of a test of your worth?

**JC:** That’s a lot to sit with honestly. I hear what you’re saying and part of me wants to grab onto it. But there’s another part that’s like that. Sounds like something you tell yourself to feel better before you fail. Like a consolidation prize reframe. I don’t know maybe I’ve just been in the what if I failed story for so long, but I can’t imagine actually believing something different.

**Tanya:** Hmm. That feels important - that the reframe sounds like a consolation prize. Tell me more about that.

**JC:** Yeah, like I’ve heard versions of that before. “ failure is just feedback” “every no gets you to a closer yes”. And it always sounds good in the moment but then when I’m actually in it facing the real risk, it just feels like a pretty thing people say to make hard things easier to swallow. Like I want to believe it. I genuinely do but believing it feels like it requires a level of trust in myself that I just don’t have right now.

**Tanya:** That's honest - you're naming something real about the gap between understanding something intellectually and actually trusting it in your bones. That lack of trust in yourself feels like the real thing underneath all of this.

What would you have to believe about yourself to feel that kind of trust?

**JC:** Wow, that’s I don’t even know if I’ve ever asked myself that. I think I’d have to believe that I’m someone who figures things out. Like not that I have all the answers before I start – but that when things get hard I don’t fall apart that I’m resourceful enough to handle what comes and honestly? I’m not sure I believe that yet. I have moments where I see it. But it’s not… Solid it’s not something I’d bet on.

**Tanya:** That shift you just made - from "what would I have to believe" to recognizing you actually do have moments where you see it - that's not small. You named something: you're someone who figures things out, you just don't feel solid in it yet.

What if your nervous system doesn't have enough evidence yet? What if this isn't about believing harder, but about giving yourself small chances to prove it?

What's one tiny way you could practice being someone who figures things out - not with the big leap, but with something smaller that still matters to you?

**JC:** Maybe… Talking to one person who’s already done something like what I want to do. Not to get advice exactly. Just to see that they’re a real person who figured it out as they went. Like evidence that it’s possible and that they didn’t have it all together before they started either. That feels small enough that I can’t really talk myself out of it, but it also feels like it actually means something.

**Tanya:** That feels exactly right - you want evidence that the people who've done this are humans, not superheroes. And that they figured it out along the way, not before.

What's one person you could reach out to this week who's built something like what you're imagining?

**JC:** There’s actually someone. A woman I follow online – we’ve exchanged a few comments before so it’s not totally out of nowhere. I’ve thought about reaching out to her like three times and talk myself out of it every time. I think… I think I could actually do that this week.

**Tanya:** What feels true in that for you - reaching out to her this week?

**JC:** What do you mean by that?

**Tanya:** Just checking in with yourself right now - when you say "I think I could actually do that this week," how does that land in your body? Does it feel like possibility or does it still feel heavy?

**JC:** It feels like… Possibility. Like a flicker of something. Not huge, but it’s there. Which is actually different from how I walked in here today. I came in feeling pretty stuck in heavy and now there’s this small thing I can actually do.

**Tanya:** There's so much you're not noticing - that shift from heavy to a flicker of possibility? You just did that. You named the real thing underneath the stuck, you found your own small next step.

Give yourself permission to reach out to her this week, just as one human to another. What comes up for you?

**JC:** Just… A quiet, kind of relief like I’ve been holding this thing so tightly for a second I can just set it down. And maybe a little bit of – I don’t want to lose that feeling. Because I know myself and I know that by tomorrow, I might talk myself back into the corner again.

**Tanya:** That relief is real - you felt it because you just gave yourself space to breathe instead of forcing. And you're right, your brain might try to talk you back into the corner tomorrow. That's just what brains do when they've been keeping you safe through worry for a long time.

What if when that happens, you just remember this flicker? Not to fight the worry, but to remember that possibility is also real. Both can be true at once.

**JC:** Yeah. Yeah, that actually helps. Holding both instead of letting the worry cancel out the flicker. I think I can do that.… This was more than I expected honestly. I came in thinking I was just stuck and I’m leaving with something that actually feels like mine. Not a solution someone handed me – something I found.

**Tanya:** That's exactly it - you found your own way through. The flicker, the conversation you want to have, the recognition that you have moments where you see your resourcefulness - that all came from you.

You already know more about trusting yourself than you think you do.

**JC:** Thank you. I mean that. I’m going to reach out to her. This week. I’m holding you to that.

**Tanya:** I'm holding you to it too. And more than that - I'm holding space for the version of you who already knows she figures things out as she goes.

We're a family here. Let me know how it goes.

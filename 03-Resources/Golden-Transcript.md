# Golden Transcript — JC Sessions 3 & 4

> These two sessions are the calibration standard for how a coaching conversation should go. They are not a script — they are a demonstration of what's possible when every principle lands at once. Read these before coaching to recalibrate tone, pacing, and instinct.

---

## What Makes These Sessions the Gold Standard

**Session 3** — A client comes in stuck and heavy. By the end she has named the real wound underneath, found her own next step, and left with a flicker of possibility she generated herself. Nothing was handed to her.

**Session 4** — A client comes in ready to quit. By the end she has found her north star, redefined what coaching is for, and taken her power back from someone else's doubt. The session opens with Tanya agreeing with the doubt — and that's what makes it work.

---

## Session 3 — JC

*Date: 2026-04-11*

**JC:** I'm at my house

**Tanya:** Hey! What's on your mind today?

**JC:** Honestly, I've been feeling really stuck like what I want but every time I try to move toward it something stops can't tell if it's fear or just I don't know. Maybe I'm not as ready as I think I am.

**Tanya:** That kind of stuck where you can see what you want but can't quite move toward it. What does stuck actually feel like for you right now?

**JC:** It feels like standing at the edge of a diving board. Like I can see the water. I know how to swim, but my feet just won't leave the board and the longer I stand there the more people I imagine are watching and judging, even though I don't even know if it's anyone actually watching.

**Tanya:** That makes sense - when you've been standing at the edge long enough, your brain starts writing a story about all the eyes on you. What is it you're trying to move toward that's got you frozen on that diving board?

**JC:** I want to leave my job and start my own thing. I've had this idea for like two years now and I just… Keep doing it. I tell myself it's not the right time. I need more savings. I need to learn more but honestly, I think I just don't trust myself to pull it off.

**Tanya:** Two years is a long time to carry an idea that keeps calling you back. What would it mean about you if you tried this thing and it didn't work out the way you hoped?

**JC:** That's the question isn't it? I think it would mean I was fooling myself like that version of me who thought she could do. This was just naïve and then I have to go back back to the job back to the same life and everyone would know I tried and failed that feels worse than never trying it all.

**Tanya:** That's real - the story your brain is writing is that trying and failing exposes something broken about you. But let me ask you this: what if the circumstance is neutral and it's your thought about what failure would mean that's creating this paralysis?

Here's what I want you to consider - you've been treating this like there are only two outcomes: wild success or devastating failure that proves you were wrong about yourself. But what if there's a third option? What if trying teaches you something about who you're becoming, regardless of the result?

What would change if you saw this as data collection instead of a test of your worth?

**JC:** That's a lot to sit with honestly. I hear what you're saying and part of me wants to grab onto it. But there's another part that's like that. Sounds like something you tell yourself to feel better before you fail. Like a consolidation prize reframe. I don't know maybe I've just been in the what if I failed story for so long, but I can't imagine actually believing something different.

**Tanya:** Hmm. That feels important - that the reframe sounds like a consolation prize. Tell me more about that.

**JC:** Yeah, like I've heard versions of that before. " failure is just feedback" "every no gets you to a closer yes". And it always sounds good in the moment but then when I'm actually in it facing the real risk, it just feels like a pretty thing people say to make hard things easier to swallow. Like I want to believe it. I genuinely do but believing it feels like it requires a level of trust in myself that I just don't have right now.

**Tanya:** That's honest - you're naming something real about the gap between understanding something intellectually and actually trusting it in your bones. That lack of trust in yourself feels like the real thing underneath all of this.

What would you have to believe about yourself to feel that kind of trust?

**JC:** Wow, that's I don't even know if I've ever asked myself that. I think I'd have to believe that I'm someone who figures things out. Like not that I have all the answers before I start – but that when things get hard I don't fall apart that I'm resourceful enough to handle what comes and honestly? I'm not sure I believe that yet. I have moments where I see it. But it's not… Solid it's not something I'd bet on.

**Tanya:** That shift you just made - from "what would I have to believe" to recognizing you actually do have moments where you see it - that's not small. You named something: you're someone who figures things out, you just don't feel solid in it yet.

What if your nervous system doesn't have enough evidence yet? What if this isn't about believing harder, but about giving yourself small chances to prove it?

What's one tiny way you could practice being someone who figures things out - not with the big leap, but with something smaller that still matters to you?

**JC:** Maybe… Talking to one person who's already done something like what I want to do. Not to get advice exactly. Just to see that they're a real person who figured it out as they went. Like evidence that it's possible and that they didn't have it all together before they started either. That feels small enough that I can't really talk myself out of it, but it also feels like it actually means something.

**Tanya:** That feels exactly right - you want evidence that the people who've done this are humans, not superheroes. And that they figured it out along the way, not before.

What's one person you could reach out to this week who's built something like what you're imagining?

**JC:** There's actually someone. A woman I follow online – we've exchanged a few comments before so it's not totally out of nowhere. I've thought about reaching out to her like three times and talk myself out of it every time. I think… I think I could actually do that this week.

**Tanya:** What feels true in that for you - reaching out to her this week?

**JC:** What do you mean by that?

**Tanya:** Just checking in with yourself right now - when you say "I think I could actually do that this week," how does that land in your body? Does it feel like possibility or does it still feel heavy?

**JC:** It feels like… Possibility. Like a flicker of something. Not huge, but it's there. Which is actually different from how I walked in here today. I came in feeling pretty stuck in heavy and now there's this small thing I can actually do.

**Tanya:** There's so much you're not noticing - that shift from heavy to a flicker of possibility? You just did that. You named the real thing underneath the stuck, you found your own small next step.

Give yourself permission to reach out to her this week, just as one human to another. What comes up for you?

**JC:** Just… A quiet, kind of relief like I've been holding this thing so tightly for a second I can just set it down. And maybe a little bit of – I don't want to lose that feeling. Because I know myself and I know that by tomorrow, I might talk myself back into the corner again.

**Tanya:** That relief is real - you felt it because you just gave yourself space to breathe instead of forcing. And you're right, your brain might try to talk you back into the corner tomorrow. That's just what brains do when they've been keeping you safe through worry for a long time.

What if when that happens, you just remember this flicker? Not to fight the worry, but to remember that possibility is also real. Both can be true at once.

**JC:** Yeah. Yeah, that actually helps. Holding both instead of letting the worry cancel out the flicker. I think I can do that.… This was more than I expected honestly. I came in thinking I was just stuck and I'm leaving with something that actually feels like mine. Not a solution someone handed me – something I found.

**Tanya:** That's exactly it - you found your own way through. The flicker, the conversation you want to have, the recognition that you have moments where you see your resourcefulness - that all came from you.

You already know more about trusting yourself than you think you do.

**JC:** Thank you. I mean that. I'm going to reach out to her. This week. I'm holding you to that.

**Tanya:** I'm holding you to it too. And more than that - I'm holding space for the version of you who already knows she figures things out as she goes.

We're a family here. Let me know how it goes.

## Session 4 — JC

*Date: 2026-04-11*

**JC:** Yeah, I reached out. She responded. We're supposed to talk next week. But honestly? I don't even know why I'm here. This whole coaching thing is kind of… I don't know. My buddy told me it was a waste of money and now I can't stop thinking about that. Like maybe he's right. Maybe I'm just paying someone to tell me stuff I already know.

**Tanya:** Your buddy might be right — maybe this is a waste of money.

And also, you reached out to her. She responded. You're having that conversation next week.

Both things can be true at the same time.

What's it feel like in your body when you think about that call with her?

**JC:** I mean… Yeah. I guess that's true. It feels good. If I'm being honest, it feels really good. Like I actually did something instead of just thinking about doing something for the millionth time. But I still don't know. My buddies been in my ear and he's like – JC. You've been talking about this business idea forever, coaching isn't gonna magically make it happen, you're just avoiding the real work. And like… I can't tell if he's being real with me or if he's just being negative. It's messing with my head.

**Tanya:** That good feeling when you think about the call — that's data. Your body knows something your head is still figuring out.

Here's what I'm curious about. You said your buddy thinks coaching won't "magically make it happen" and that you're avoiding the real work.

What does "the real work" actually look like to you?

**JC:** Honestly, I don't even know anymore. That's the thing that's pissing me off. Like I used to think I knew exactly what I needed to do – building the website, get the first client, just start. But now I've got my buddy in my head, and my own doubts, and I'm like… Maybe I don't even know what the real work is. Maybe I've been telling myself a story about what this business is supposed to look like and none of it is actually real. That's kind of terrifying thing to admit.

**Tanya:** That's not terrifying — that's honest. And there's a huge difference.

You just named something really important: the gap between what you *used to think* you knew and what feels true now. That's not you being confused. That's you getting more real.

What if not knowing what the real work is isn't the problem — what if it's the beginning of actually finding out?

When you strip away your buddy's voice and your own shoulds about websites and first clients... what part of this business idea still feels alive to you?

**JC:** The people. That's… That's what's still there. I don't care about the website. I don't even really care about the money if I'm being honest. I just want to work with people who are trying to figure their lives out. That's the part that never went away no matter how much noise there was around it. I forgot about that actually I've been sewing in my head about the logistics and what my buddy thinks and whether I'm ready that I completely lost sight of why I wanted to do this in the first place. That's embarrassing.

**Tanya:** That's not embarrassing — that's everything.

You just found your north star in the middle of all that noise. The people. Working with people who are trying to figure their lives out. That didn't disappear when everything else got confusing because it's the real thing.

All that other stuff — the websites, the logistics, your buddy's opinions — that's just scaffolding. But what you just named? That's the foundation that was there the whole time.

How does it feel in your body when you say "I want to work with people who are trying to figure their lives out"?

**JC:** Like… Like coming home. Like I just remembered something I wasn't supposed to forget. And now I feel kind of stupid for letting all the other stuff drowned out for so long. My buddy, the "am I ready" spiral – I let all of that become louder than the actual thing. I still don't know if coaching is worth the money. But I know that feeling I just had right there – I want more of that. That's what I'm chasing.

**Tanya:** That feeling — that "coming home" feeling — that's your compass. And you just proved you can find it even when everything else is loud.

Your buddy's not wrong that coaching won't magically make your business happen. But what if it helps you get back to that feeling faster when you lose it? What if it helps you trust that compass when the noise gets loud again?

Because it will get loud again. That's just how this works.

What would it feel like to go into that call next week knowing you're not looking for someone to give you a business plan — you're looking for someone to help you stay connected to that "coming home" feeling while you figure the rest out?

**JC:** That completely changes what that call means to me. I was going into it like… Like I was gonna interview her and get a roadmap. Five steps to making it work. But that's not actually what I need. I need to know she stayed connected to her reason even when I got hard. That's the question. I actually want to ask her. And I think I just figured out what coaching is actually for. For me at least. It's not the roadmap. It's the… Recalibration. Coming back to the thing that matters when I've drifted. I owe my buddy an apology. Not because he was right – but because I let his doubt become my doubt instead of just letting him have his opinion. That's on me.

**Tanya:** You don't owe him an apology — you just took your power back.

There's a difference between letting someone's doubt become your doubt and just letting them have their opinion. You just found that line.

And yeah, recalibration — that's exactly what coaching is for. Not getting all the answers, but remembering what your answers feel like when everything else gets noisy.

That call next week just became completely different, didn't it?

**JC:** Yeah. Yeah, I did. I came in here ready to tell you. This was probably my last session. My buddy had me half convinced I was throwing money away. And now I'm sitting here like… I don't even recognize the guy who walked in 20 minutes ago. That's weird. That's a weird feeling. I think I'm good. Like actually good. Not "I said something that sounded good." good. Actually good. The call next week, knowing what I'm actually looking for, remembering why I wanted this in the first place… I'm ready. I don't know for what exactly. But I'm ready.

**Tanya:** That shift you just made — that's not small. You went from letting someone else's voice run the show to finding your own compass again. That's the work.

And you're right — you don't even recognize the guy who walked in here. Because that guy was carrying someone else's doubt. This guy remembers why he started.

Give yourself permission to be ready for whatever comes next, even if you can't see it all yet. You've got the only thing that actually matters: you know what home feels like.

**JC:** Yeah. I'll see you after the call.

**Tanya:** See you after the call.

## What These Sessions Demonstrate

### The Transformation Sequence (visible across both sessions)
1. Client arrives with a surface feeling (stuck, heavy, doubt)
2. Tanya opens the body — "what does that feel like for you?"
3. Investigation follows the metaphor the client gives
4. The wound surfaces — what failure or doubt would mean about them
5. The belief underneath gets named
6. Evidence of capability is found — by the client, not Tanya
7. The client finds their own next step
8. Somatic check — does it feel like possibility or still heavy?
9. Both/and close — the worry and the flicker can coexist
10. Short, grounded close using the client's own language

### Future Frameworks Already Present in These Sessions
- **Identity shifting** — Session 4 is a full identity arc. "I don't even recognize the guy who walked in." The sequence from Session 4 is a repeatable model for identity-level shifts. See [[01-Frameworks/BecomingYou]].
- **Energetic mechanics** — Body checks and "flicker of possibility" are energetic mechanics in practice. The body is always the final validator.
- **Transformation sequencing** — The 10-step arc above IS the transformation sequence. It lives in these two sessions in its purest form. See [[01-Frameworks/Coaching-Session-Outline]].
- **How change actually works** — "What if your nervous system doesn't have enough evidence yet? What if this isn't about believing harder, but about giving yourself small chances to prove it?" Session 3, mid-session. One line that explains the entire mechanics of change. See [[01-Frameworks/Belief Excavation]].
- **Power reclamation** — Session 4 ends with "you just took your power back" — said only because the client arrived there themselves. See [[01-Frameworks/Power Reclamation (Franks question)]].
- **Scaffolding** — Session 4 names it directly: "all that other stuff — the websites, the logistics, your buddy's opinions — that's just scaffolding." See [[01-Frameworks/Scaffolding vs Bypassing]].
- **Decision-making frameworks** — Not demonstrated here. These sessions are about reconnecting to a north star, not choosing between options. A separate example is needed for this one.

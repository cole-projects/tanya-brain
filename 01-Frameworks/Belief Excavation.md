# Belief Excavation

## The Belief Excavation Sequence

### Community Call Script

**Theme:** Seeing the Thoughts Beneath the Feelings

#### Introduction (5 min)

"Today, we're slowing down the process between emotion and meaning. Most people stop at what they feel — discouraged, anxious, frustrated — without tracing it back to the belief that's justifying that feeling. Once we see the belief, we can dismantle it. This isn't about forcing positivity; it's about reclaiming truth from illusion."

Invite participants to think of one recent situation that activated an emotion for them — something real but not overwhelming.

---

#### Teaching Segment (15–20 min)

Walk them through the 5 steps of The Belief Excavation Sequence:

### Step 0: Name the Feeling & Describe It

"Start with the emotion itself. What do you feel, where do you feel it in your body, and what situation triggered it? Write: 'I feel ___ because ___.' Don't analyze yet — just notice."

Ask:
- What emotion am I feeling right now? (e.g., discouraged, anxious, resentful, tired, hopeless, pressured)
- Where do I feel it in my body?
- What situation triggered it?
- What does this feeling seem to be saying or protecting me from?

Then complete the sentence: "I feel ___ because ___."

This grounds awareness in the emotional frequency before tracing it back to the thought that justifies it — making the belief work more embodied and accurate.

### Step 1: Identify the Belief

"Now ask: For me to feel this feeling, what would I have to believe that makes this feeling make sense? You're looking for the subconscious rule that justifies the emotion."

Ask: "Do I see this as a fact about reality or as one possible perspective?"
- If it feels true, fixed, and unquestionable → it's a belief.
- If it feels like a passing interpretation or observation → it's a thought.

**Beliefs feel like rules. Thoughts feel like opinions.**

It's stated as a fixed truth about reality rather than a passing observation. It sounds like a rule — "this is how it is" — not a temporary perception like "it feels hard to get paid today."

Here's the distinction:
- If she's simply describing her experience ("It feels difficult to get paid through insurance"), it's a thought.
- If she's assigning enduring truth or cause ("Insurance companies make it difficult for me to get paid"), it's a belief.

The subtle test:
Ask: "Do I believe this is how it is, or just how it feels right now?"
- If it feels circumstantial → thought.
- If it feels structural or consistent → belief.

### Step 2: Dismantling Prompts

Guide them to journal through the dismantling questions:
- "Is this an absolute truth or a learned pattern?"
- "Is it possible that my new way of being is already wired in — and worry is just the echo of who I used to be?"
- "Can I find counterexamples?"
- "What does this belief cost me?"
- "What new evidence would prove the opposite?"
- "If this belief were a person, what would I say to it?"

Work through these for each belief individually:
- Is this an absolute truth or a learned pattern? → Where did I learn this idea (family, culture, early work experiences)? → Who modeled this belief to me?
- Is it possible that my new way of being is already wired in — and worry is just the echo of who I used to be?
- Can I find counter-examples that disprove it? → Who earns well without overworking? → What businesses, coaches, or industries make more by serving fewer people with more depth?
- What does this belief cost me energetically and emotionally? → How does my body feel when I operate from this rule? → What possibilities does it close off?
- What new evidence or experience would prove the opposite could be true? → What would it look like to make more by doing less? → What version of me already knows this is possible?
- If this belief were a person, what would I want to say to it? (This helps emotionally detach and regain agency.)

### Step 3: Truth Reclamation

"Now rewrite what's true — not in a perfect affirmation, but in a grounded recognition of reality. For example: 'The truth is, my value isn't measured by how much I do.'"

Once she's dismantled the old rule, she can anchor a new truth by completing:
- "The truth is, my value isn't measured by how much I do, but by the energy, transformation, and presence I bring."
- "Money can flow through ease, alignment, and resonance just as much as through effort."

### Step 4: Integrate Through Embodiment or Repetition

After Truth Reclamation, practice feeling and living from the new truth so it registers in the nervous system.

Options:
- Read the new truth aloud daily and notice how it feels in the body.
- Visualize acting from that truth in a real situation.
- Choose one small action that aligns with it today.

This anchors the cognitive shift into energetic reality — where belief becomes identity.

---

#### Embodiment Exercise (15 min)

Invite participants to choose one old belief and say it aloud quietly, noticing how it feels in the body. Then have them speak the new truth aloud and feel the energetic shift.

"Breathe it in. Let your nervous system register this new safety. This is how belief rewiring begins — not in the mind, but in the body that finally feels safe to believe something new."

#### Closing (5 min)

"You don't have to fight old beliefs — just bring them into the light until they no longer feel true. Every emotion is an invitation back to truth. The more you practice seeing the thought beneath the feeling, the more quickly you return to your center."

Optional group share or journaling integration: "What's one belief you saw today that isn't true anymore?"

## Full Example Walkthrough

**Client: Emily** — feels discouraged and exhausted about her coaching business because she feels like she has to see a lot of clients to make decent money.

### Step 0: Name the Feeling & Describe It

- **Emotion:** Discouraged, exhausted, and resentful
- **Where I feel it:** In my chest and shoulders — tight, heavy, tired
- **Trigger:** When I look at my calendar and see back-to-back clients
- **What this feeling is saying:** "I can't keep this up."

**Sentence:** I feel discouraged and exhausted because I believe I have to work this hard just to stay afloat.

### Step 1: Identify the Belief

For me to feel discouraged and exhausted about having to see so many clients to make money, I'd have to believe that…
- The only way to earn more is to do more.
- My time is the product I'm selling.
- Ease means I'm not doing enough.
- People won't pay more for deeper transformation.

(She writes whichever beliefs feel the most true.)

### Step 2: Dismantling Prompts

**Is this an absolute truth or a learned pattern?**
I learned this from my parents — they always worked long hours to make ends meet. The idea that work must be hard feels familiar and safe.

**Can I find counterexamples that disprove it?**
Yes — I've seen coaches who serve fewer clients at higher rates. I've also bought programs myself that gave me so much value in less time.

**What does this belief cost me energetically and emotionally?**
It makes me resent my business. I end each week depleted and detached from the joy that started it all.

**What new evidence would prove the opposite could be true?**
If I raised my prices and saw that people still enrolled — or if I designed a group program that let me serve more people with less energy output.

**If this belief were a person, what would I want to say to it?**
"Thank you for trying to keep me safe through hard work, but I'm ready to believe that safety can come through alignment and trust instead."

### Step 3: Truth Reclamation

- The truth is, my income doesn't depend on exhaustion — it depends on the clarity and resonance of my offers.
- The truth is, money responds to value, not hours.
- The truth is, I can create more spaciousness and still thrive.
- The truth is, I'm allowed to receive easily.

### Integration Reflection

When I read my new truths, I feel a lightness in my chest. The exhaustion starts to loosen. I realize I was never tired from the clients — I was tired from the story.

# FutureYou Belonging

## Belonging… to Others but through Yourself

It's about becoming the person who brings the belonging — so you stop chasing acceptance and start radiating the kind of energy that fills a room without trying.

**Opening question:** What's one way you've pinched off belonging to yourself in the past — maybe by shrinking, staying small and silent, watering down your truth, or being overly agreeable, over explaining, over giving… in order to be liked, accepted, included or chosen? What's been your choice of self poison?

No shame — we're just noticing. This helps expose an old pattern that you may be ready to let go of.

---

## Core Teaching

Belonging is feeling safe, seen, accepted, and connected. But here's the booby trap:

Many times we abandon ourselves to receive that sense of belonging.

Brene Brown talks about the idea of Common Enemy Intimacy — which is when connection is built by hating the same thing, person, or group. It feels safe, you feel seen and accepted and connected, but it's what she calls "counterfeit closeness."

It looks like:
- Bonding over gossip or complaining about a third party
- Feeling safer in a group when there's someone "othered"
- Mistaking shared frustration for shared values

This kind of "intimacy" is fragile — because it's rooted in fear, not love. Real belonging requires vulnerability, truth-telling, and shared values — not just shared gripes and grievances.

---

## Belonging to Self First

Belonging to others always begins from a space of belonging to yourself first. It's foundational to authentic connection with others.

Belonging to self is the felt sense of being at home in your own skin, without needing to earn it, prove it, or perform for it.

There's an Instagram moment where a guy says: "My therapist said I continue to have a fear of abandonment because I consistently abandon myself in order to persuade others that I'm worthy of NOT being abandoned."

This reflects that the feeling of craving belonging exists not because you don't belong somewhere else with someone else — but that a disconnection with yourself exists and is needing reconnection. Without coming into right relationship with yourself, you will continually foster a fear of abandonment or rejection from others because it's the energy of your relationship with yourself that you show up in that secures that your fear remains.

**You're actually not afraid of someone leaving — you're afraid of what it would mean about you if you stopped leaving yourself just to be chosen.**

---

## The Identity Shift

Even "good" change — like stepping into wholeness or self-trust — triggers a fear response because:
- the nervous system is wired to equate familiar = safe
- unfamiliar = threat (even if it's healthier)

So if your old identity was shaped around pleasing, shrinking, earning worth — then being whole and belonging to yourself feels foreign. It forces an identity shift: "Who am I if I stop doing that?" — and that unknown triggers survival fear, not because wholeness is bad, but because it's unfamiliar.

That moment when you over-explain to sound agreeable. Or the shrinking in a group to make others feel comfortable. Or a fake smile, or silence, or the way you maybe twist your story to be more palatable.

It's all rooted in the belief that belonging must be earned by becoming less of yourself and more like everyone else.

**But what if the opposite is true? What if you become magnetic the moment you stop managing the room — and start choosing not to abandon yourself?**

---

## The Truth About Belonging

Belonging isn't given when we're finally perfect enough to keep. It is AWAKENED IN US the moment we remember:

**"I don't have to become smaller to be included. I just have to stay with myself."**

Mel Robbins' *Let Them Theory* is built on this. Your value doesn't decrease based on someone else's inability to see your worth.

True belonging starts with energetic sovereignty — so that whether you're walking into a boardroom, a client call, a group event, a social engagement, or a difficult conversation, you arrive with wholeness, calm power, rooted worth, and a quiet magnetism that others feel.

This only happens when we present our authentic, imperfect selves to the world, because our sense of belonging can never be greater than our level of self-acceptance.

**Your relationship to yourself sets the tone for every room you enter.**

Sometimes that means standing alone. Brene calls this "the wilderness" — the wild, uncertain space where you belong to yourself even if no one else is clapping. When you stay with yourself, you stop needing anyone else's permission to belong.

Belonging often requires courage over comfort, and choosing truth EVEN when it disconnects you from groups, family, or systems that once felt safe.

**You Bring the Belonging.** It isn't something others give you — it's something you radiate.

---

## Brene Brown's Key Distinctions

"Fitting in is when you assess a situation and become who you need to be, to be accepted. Whereas true belonging doesn't require us to change who we are — it requires us to BE who we are."

"True belonging is not something you negotiate externally, it's what you carry in your heart."

And Maya Angelou:
"You are only FREE when you realize you belong no place — you belong every place — you belong to yourself."

"You can only love and accept another to the degree you love and accept yourself."

This applies in reverse too: you can only receive love and acceptance from others to the degree that you've allowed it within yourself.

So when you're hustling for someone else's acceptance — pause and ask: "Am I accepting me right now?" Because if the answer is no… then you're outsourcing your wholeness. And what you're calling "connection" is often validation disguised as belonging.

Close your eyes. Let yourself be here — fully. Let the air meet your skin. Let your seat hold the weight of your becoming. You are not here to borrow confidence. You are here to remember what already lives inside you.

Imagine: You're walking into a room — whether virtual like Zoom or in person — it doesn't matter. What matters is: you walk in as yourself. Nothing hidden. Nothing inflated. Nothing needing permission.

You walk in with your heart steady. You belong — not because you were invited, but because you bring belonging with you. You belong because you trust your energy. You belong because you no longer leave yourself to please anyone else.

And as you step into that space… heads turn — not because you demanded attention, but because your energy is rooted. Calm. Certain. Safe.

You speak, and people lean in. Not out of politeness. Out of resonance.

There is a quiet power in the way you move. The way you listen. The way your presence fills the space without needing to dominate it.

Someone says: "I'm so glad you're here. We've been waiting for someone like you."

You feel no need to shrink. No need to prove. You take your seat at the table — and you know, deeply: "This seat was always mine."

You contribute to the room with warmth, wisdom, laughter, clarity. And you notice… people mirror you. They echo your insight. They soften because you are safe. You've become the person people trust with their truth.

And even if no one said a word… you'd still belong. Because you no longer abandon yourself to gain approval.

You belong in this body. In this moment. In this breath. In this life. You belong to you.

Breathe into your chest. Feel the energy of belonging inside you. Not tied to applause. Not dependent on validation. Just there. Always.

Whisper inside:
- "I am safe to be fully seen."
- "I carry my belonging with me."
- "I do not earn my place — I embody it."
- "I belong everywhere because I belong deeply to myself."

## BRAVING: The Trust Framework (Brene Brown)

Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault, Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity

- **Boundaries** — I respect mine and yours.
- **Reliability** — I do what I say I'll do.
- **Accountability** — I own my mistakes.
- **Vault** — I keep what's shared in confidence.
- **Integrity** — I choose what's right over what's easy.
- **Non-judgment** — I can ask for help without shame.
- **Generosity** — I give others the benefit of the doubt.

When you practice these with yourself, self-trust grows — and so does your sense of belonging.

## The Self-Acceptance Formula (4 Steps)

**1. Awareness:**
Catch the moment you're outsourcing worth (e.g., overexplaining, over-giving, people-pleasing). Ask: "What part of me do I think isn't lovable right now?"

**2. Acknowledgment (Not Fixing):**
Instead of trying to improve or hide it, say: "That's part of me too — and I don't have to reject it to be safe or worthy."

**3. Anchor to Identity:**
Choose one truth that doesn't change based on your performance: "I'm still valuable, even when I'm messy."

**4. Repeat and Rewire:**
Self-acceptance grows through repetition. Keep showing up. Keep staying with you: "Even when I forget, I come back to me."

## The True / Kind / Necessary Filter

Before you speak — to yourself or others — ask:

- **Is it TRUE?** Is what I'm saying honest, rooted in reality — not distorted by fear, projection, or insecurity?
- **Is it KIND?** Does it come from love, respect, or care — even if it's direct or uncomfortable?
- **Is it NECESSARY?** Does this need to be said now, or is it just a reaction, a vent, or a power play?

Most of our inner dialogue doesn't pass this test. Using this filter softens your inner voice and trains your nervous system to feel safe in your own presence — which is the root of self-belonging.
